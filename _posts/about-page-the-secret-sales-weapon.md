Let’s be honest. When you look at the web traffic stats for your business website, you’ll notice a funny trend. After your homepage, the most visited page on your entire site is almost always the "About Us" page. 

But if you look at what’s actually written on most small business About pages, it’s enough to put a hyperactive toddler to sleep. It’s usually a dry, robotic biography filled with dates, degrees, and corporate fluff: *"Founded in 2014, our mission is to leverage synergies to maximize quality outcomes..."* 

When a potential customer clicks your About page, they aren't looking for a corporate history lesson. They are asking one simple, selfish question: **"Does this person actually understand my problem, and can they help me?"**

If your page reads like a dry resume, you’re missing out on a massive sales opportunity. Let’s look at how to flip your About page on its head and turn it into a quiet sales machine—without sounding like a slimy infomercial.

---

## 1. The Structure Shift: Put the Customer First

Most people start their About page with themselves. We need to flip that. Start with the *buyer’s* problem, then introduce your origin story as the answer to that problem. 

To do this easily, we use a simple copywriting formula called **PAS**. 

> *“PAS” stands for Problem, Agitate, Solution. It’s a classic writing framework designed to grab a reader’s attention by showing them you understand exactly what they are going through before you offer your fix.*

* **Problem:** State the clear, everyday headache your customer faces.
* **Agitate:** Paint a picture of how frustrating that headache gets if it isn't fixed.
* **Solution:** Introduce your business as the relief valve.

### A Quick Before-and-After Example

Imagine you run a local commercial commercial landscaping firm called GreenScapes. 

* **The Old Way:** *"GreenScapes was founded in 2018. We have a fleet of five mowers and are dedicated to green excellence in the tri-county area."*
* **The PAS Way:** *"Most property managers we talk to are sick of dealing with landscapers who show up late, leave gate keys unlatched, and miss spots on the lawn. It makes you look bad to your tenants and wastes hours of your week playing phone tag. We started GreenScapes to fix that. We guarantee a dedicated crew leader on your property at the same time every single week—or your service is free."*

See the difference? The second version sells your reliability because it focuses entirely on what the buyer is worried about.

## 2. Ditch the Stock Photos (Use the 45% Rule)

We’ve all seen them: those perfectly polished stock photos of people in blindingly white teeth and crisp suits pretending to look at a tablet together. 

Your customers can spot fake stock photos from a mile away, and it instantly triggers their "skepticism radar." 

A well-known website testing group ran a study comparing generic stock imagery against real photos of actual team members. The results were stark: **web pages featuring real photos of the people behind the business saw a 45% lift in trust scores and conversions.** 

You don't need to pay thousands of dollars for a high-end magazine photoshoot. 

* Grab a modern smartphone.
* Go outside in natural daylight.
* Take a clean, smiling, shoulders-up headshot of yourself and your core team members in your everyday work attire. 

When a local buyer sees a real human face, they feel an immediate connection. They realize they are dealing with a neighbor, not a faceless corporation.

## 3. Write a "Hero Letter"

Somewhere in the middle of your About page, insert a short, standalone section that looks like a personal note from you, the owner. We call this a **Hero Letter**. 

This shouldn’t read like marketing material. It should read exactly like an email you would write to a stressed-out friend who needs your professional help. Keep it to one short paragraph.

> *Example:* 
> "Hey there, I'm Mike. I spent 12 years working in big corporate auto shops, and I got tired of seeing regular people get talked down to and overcharged for simple brake fixes. That’s why I opened my own garage. When you bring your truck here, we’ll explain what’s wrong in plain English, show you the parts, and never push a repair you don't actually need. That's my personal promise."

## 4. Drop in Seamless "Proof Placement"

You need to show you know your stuff, but you don’t want to brag. Instead of creating a giant, separate wall of bragging rights, weave your proof directly into the story.

> *“Proof placement” simply means dropping short, real-world examples of your success—like data points or brief customer quotes—right into your writing to back up your claims naturally.*

A great way to do this is by stating a fact right after your solution sentence. 

* *How to weave it in:* "We built an automated invoicing system for local plumbers because we knew how painful chasing paper checks can be. **In fact, just last month, we helped Dave over at Apex Plumbing get paid $14,000 in outstanding bills in less than a week.**"

This feels like a natural part of the conversation, but it subtly sends a powerful message to the reader: *This actually works.*

## 5. Add a 30-Second Video Invitation

If you want to completely crush your competition, record a short video greeting and embed it right at the top or middle of your About page. 

People buy from people they know, like, and trust. A video lets them hear your voice, see your enthusiasm, and gauge your sincerity before they ever pick up the phone. 

`[Video: A short example of an owner-shot smartphone video showing a welcoming, 30-second introduction that invites visitors to book a call]`

**What to say in your 30-second video:**

1. **0-10 seconds:** Say hello and state who you help. *"Hi, I'm Brenda. Welcome to Oakwood Bookkeeping. We help local bakery and cafe owners make sense of their monthly numbers."*
2. **10-20 seconds:** Validate their headache. *"We know you went into business to bake incredible food, not to spend your Sunday nights crying over spreadsheets."*
3. **20-30 seconds:** Direct call to action. *"Take a look around this page, and when you're ready, hit the 'Free Consultation' button at the top to see how we can take that paperwork off your plate."*

## The Common Mistake: The "Timeline" Trap

The absolute most common mistake on small business About pages is the chronological timeline. It usually looks like a list of bullet points:

* *2014: Opened our doors in a small office.*
* *2017: Expanded our team to 4 people.*
* *2020: Won the regional business badge.*

Unless you are a historic 150-year-old institution where longevity is your primary selling point, **delete the timeline.** Your customers don't care what year you upgraded from a laptop to a desktop. They care about what you can do for them today.

## Your Quick About Page Checklist

- [ ] The first two paragraphs focus entirely on the customer's problem, not your history.
- [ ] There is a real, high-quality photo of the owner or team (no stock models).
- [ ] The language is casual—read it out loud to ensure it sounds like a normal conversation.
- [ ] A short "Hero Letter" explains the personal *why* behind the business.
- [ ] At least one concrete data point or mini-story proves you’ve solved this problem before.

---

## When This Advice Doesn't Apply

This conversational, human-first approach is incredibly effective for small to mid-sized service businesses, local contractors, consultants, and family-owned shops. 

However, if you are bidding on massive government defense contracts, or dealing with highly rigid corporate compliance boards that require strict legal disclosures and technical vetting docs, an informal "Hero Letter" might feel out of place to an enterprise procurement committee. In those highly regulated industries, you’ll need a more formal corporate profile page. For everyone else selling to regular business owners and local clients, keeping it human is your unfair advantage.
