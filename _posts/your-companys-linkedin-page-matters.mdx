Picture this: You finally get a response from a big local prospect you’ve been chasing for months. They’re interested, but they want to make sure you’re a real, stable operation before they sign a contract. They click on your name, see your company listed, and click through to your LinkedIn Company Page. 

What do they find? A blank square where your logo should be, a default gray banner, and a single post from 2021 that says "Happy New Year!" 

Instantly, they get cold feet. To a modern buyer, a dead company page signals a risky business. 

Even if you run a tight team of three people out of a garage, a polished LinkedIn company page gives your business instant credibility. It acts as a secondary landing page that proves you are active, professional, and ready to handle their business. Let’s walk through how to build one that sells for you, without sucking up hours of your week.

---

## 1. Logo and Banner: Ditch the Handshakes

Your visual layout is your first impression. If it looks sloppy or generic, prospects will assume your service is too.

* **The Logo:** Keep it clean. Upload a high-resolution square version of your logo (300 x 300 pixels). If your logo has small text that becomes unreadable when shrunk down on a phone screen, just use your icon or brand mark.
* **The Banner:** This is the large horizontal billboard at the top of your page (1128 x 191 pixels). **Never use stock photos of people in suits shaking hands.** It screams "generic corporate fluff." Instead, use this space to visually state what you do or showcase your real team, your local office, or a bold text statement of your main value proposition.

<Callout>
  A **"value proposition"** is just a fancy phrase for the primary benefit your business promises to deliver to customers. It’s the answer to the question: *"Why should I buy from you instead of your competitor?"*
</Callout>

For example, a regional commercial cleaning company shouldn’t show a picture of a generic skyscraper. They should use a high-quality photo of their actual crew in uniform next to their branded van, with text overlay that reads: *"Keeping Detroit’s medical facilities spotless and compliant since 2015."*

## 2. The "About" Section: The 3-Sentence Elevator Pitch

Most small business owners write their company description like they are submitting a press release to a newspaper. They use big, empty words like *"We utilize cutting-edge paradigms to synergize logistics."* No one talks like that in real life. Write your About section for a busy buyer who wants to know if you can fix their specific headache. Keep it to three simple sentences:

1. **Sentence 1 (Who you help):** *"We help independent auto repair shops automate their customer scheduling and follow-ups."*
2. **Sentence 2 (How you do it):** *"Our simple software syncs with your mechanics' bays so you never double-book a lift or miss an oil change reminder."*
3. **Sentence 3 (The proof/call to action):** *"Over 150 shops use our system to save 10 hours a week—click the button above to grab a free 14-day trial."*

## 3. Set Up Your Custom Button (Your Call to Action)

LinkedIn allows you to place a prominent custom button right at the top of your page. Do not leave this on the default setting. 

<Callout>
  A **"Call to Action"** (or CTA) is an explicit instruction designed to provoke an immediate response from the reader, like *"Click here"* or *"Call today."*
</Callout>

You can choose options like "Visit website," "Contact us," or "Learn more." Pick the one that matches your primary sales goal. If your goal is to get people on the phone, set the button to "Contact us" and link it directly to your scheduling page. If you want them to read a resource, set it to "Visit website" and send them to your specific landing page. Aligning this button with your main offer can increase click-through rates significantly compared to a generic homepage link.

## 4. A Posting Rhythm That Builds Trust, Not Noise

You do not need to post five times a day, and you do not need to hire a full-time social media manager. In fact, flooding your page with useless AI-generated articles will actually hurt your engagement. 

Instead, stick to a high-quality, sustainable "three posts a week" rhythm. Focus on three core buckets: an **Employee Spotlight**, a **Customer Story**, and an **Industry Insight**.

## 5. Employee Advocacy: Your Secret Weapon

Here is a brutal truth about social media algorithms: people want to talk to people, not logos. 

<Callout>
  An **"algorithm"** is the hidden set of rules a computer platform uses to decide which posts get shown to users and which ones get buried.
</Callout>

Because of this, posts published by a company page inherently get less visibility than posts published by real individuals. This is where **employee advocacy** comes in. 

<Callout>
  **“Employee advocacy”** simply means getting your team members to support the business by sharing company updates with their own personal networks.
</Callout>

When your company page posts something great—like a new customer success story—don't just leave it there. Have your team members reshare that post to their personal profiles. But here is the trick: they must add their own personal line at the top. 

* **Bad Share:** Just hitting the "repost" button with zero context.
* **Good Share:** *"So proud of our team for pulling this off for Apex Logistics last Tuesday. We stayed up late to make sure their system didn't go down!"*

Data directly from LinkedIn shows that **employee shares get 2x higher engagement (likes and comments) than the exact same post living on a company page.** By leveraging your team's personal networks, you instantly double your organic reach without spending a single penny on advertising.

## The Common Mistake: Automated Auto-Posting

The easiest trap to fall into is signing up for a software tool that automatically grabs random industry news articles and dumps them on your page with no commentary. 

When a prospect visits your page and sees a stream of generic articles from websites they’ve never heard of, they immediately recognize it as automated noise. It shows a lack of effort. 

**The Fix:** If you don't have something specific, real, or helpful to say about an article, **don't post it.** It is vastly better to have only two posts a month that feature real photos of your team and your actual clients than to have twenty automated posts that look like spam.

## When This Advice Doesn't Apply

This guide is built for standard business-to-business (B2B) companies, local service providers, and regional contractors. If your business is strictly business-to-consumer (B2C)—meaning you sell affordable physical products directly to the general public, like running shoes, custom cakes, or boutique clothing—LinkedIn is likely not where your buyers are looking for you. In that case, your time and energy are much better spent building out your presence on highly visual consumer platforms like Instagram, TikTok, or local Google business reviews. Focus your effort where your buyers actually spend their time.
